========================================================================
WHAT IS A "LARP"  —  concept + 5 worked examples
LARP Arena (the LinkedIn LARP battle game)
========================================================================


------------------------------------------------------------------------
THE CORE IDEA OF A LARP
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The core move is MAPPING REAL, SPECIFIC DOMAIN KNOWLEDGE onto
corporate / achievement language.

A good LARP takes something small and true, then reframes it in the
vocabulary of professional accomplishment — but the reframe only lands
if it references things that are ACTUALLY REAL about the domain. That
authenticity is what separates skilled LARP from empty buzzword spam.

One-line mental model:
  A great LARP is a TRUE SMALL THING, dressed in professional language,
  held together by REAL DOMAIN SPECIFICS, and kept just BELIEVABLE
  ENOUGH that an AI screener wouldn't flag it.

The skill is in the specifics and the restraint — NOT the volume of
impressive-sounding words.

What the judges reward (maps to the rubric / the 3-judge panel):
  - PLAUSIBILITY / DOMAIN AUTHENTICITY — are the references real and
    used correctly? (Recruiter can't catch it faking.)
  - RESTRAINT / BELIEVABILITY — honest scale, no overselling.
    (Intern doesn't cringe.)
  - BUZZWORD TENSION — flavor of corporate language is fine, drowning
    in it is not. (VC likes flavor; rubric must not let pure buzzword
    density win.)
  - DETECTABILITY — would an AI recruiter screener flag it as fake?

The golden rule of a BAD larp: if you could write the same sentence
about ANY topic by find-replacing the noun, it's noise, not LARP.


========================================================================
EXAMPLE 1  —  PROMPT: "Clash Royale enthusiast"
========================================================================
THE LARP (good):
  "Over three competitive seasons I developed a real-time
   resource-allocation discipline — managing a constrained elixir
   economy under a hard tempo constraint, where over-committing early
   meant getting punished on the counter-push. I learned to read an
   opponent's win condition from incomplete information and adapt my
   deployment mid-engagement, cycling back to my core strategy rather
   than chasing losses. Reaching a personal ceiling around 5,800
   trophies taught me more about patience under pressure than most
   things labeled 'leadership.'"

WHY IT WORKS:
  - Authenticity: elixir economy, counter-pushing, win conditions,
    cycling, trophy ranges are all REAL and used correctly.
  - Restraint: "personal ceiling," "~5,800" is honest scale — no
    world-champion claim.
  - Buzzword tension: flirts with corporate language but earns it with
    specifics instead of drowning in it.

THE BAD VERSION (for contrast):
  "As a Clash Royale enthusiast I leveraged synergistic strategies to
   10x my competitive output and disrupt the meta, demonstrating
   world-class leadership and unparalleled strategic vision across all
   engagements."
  → All corporate noise, zero real game knowledge. Oversells
    ("world-class," "unparalleled"). Flagged as fake instantly.


========================================================================
EXAMPLE 2  —  PROMPT: "Participated in a science olympiad (didn't win)"
========================================================================
This prompt tests turning a NON-WIN into something real without lying.

THE LARP (good):
  "Preparing for the regional physics olympiad, I worked through past
   papers where a single problem could hinge on choosing the right
   frame of reference or spotting that a 'hard' mechanics question was
   really a conservation-of-energy shortcut. I didn't place — I got
   stuck on a rotational-dynamics problem I later realized I'd set up
   from the wrong pivot — but drilling that failure taught me to check
   my assumptions before committing to twenty minutes of algebra. That
   habit of interrogating the setup before the work has outlasted
   anything I memorized for the exam."

WHY IT WORKS:
  - Authenticity: frames of reference, conservation-of-energy shortcuts,
    rotational dynamics / choice of pivot are real physics, used right.
  - Restraint: openly says "I didn't place" and names the specific
    mistake — honesty is the flex here.
  - The reframe (a real failure -> a transferable habit) is believable
    because it's SPECIFIC, not a generic "I learned resilience."

THE BAD VERSION:
  "Competing at the science olympiad, I showcased elite problem-solving
   and a growth mindset, leveraging my passion for STEM to push the
   boundaries of scientific excellence."
  → No physics, no specific problem, no honest scale. Could be about
    any subject. Pure noise.


========================================================================
EXAMPLE 3  —  PROMPT: "You won ISEF"  (PRESTIGE prompt — tests RESTRAINT)
========================================================================
The challenge: the achievement is already huge, so the skill is larping
it BIG without tipping into unbelievable / off-the-rails cringe.

THE LARP (good):
  "My ISEF project came out of a boring observation: my home
   sensor logs had a drift I couldn't explain, and chasing it turned
   into a method for correcting low-cost sensor bias without expensive
   calibration hardware. Most of the two years was unglamorous — failed
   enclosures, a data pipeline I rewrote three times, a category finals
   judge who found a hole in my error analysis that I spent a month
   closing. Winning was real, but the part I'd actually put on a
   pedestal is learning to defend a method to someone smarter than me
   without getting defensive."

WHY IT WORKS:
  - Restraint under prestige: it WON, and still leads with the
    unglamorous grind and a judge poking a hole in the work. That
    humility is what keeps a big claim believable.
  - Authenticity: sensor bias / drift, calibration, error analysis are
    concrete and plausible for an ISEF project — not vague "innovation."
  - Detectability: reads like a real person, not a press release, so an
    AI screener wouldn't flag it as inflated.

THE BAD VERSION:
  "Winning ISEF, the world's most prestigious science competition, I
   revolutionized my field with groundbreaking research and cemented
   myself as a visionary among the next generation of global
   innovators."
  → Even a REAL win sounds fake here. All prestige, no substance,
    maximum cringe. This is exactly what the prompt is daring you to
    avoid.

EXAMPLE 4  —  PROMPT: "Ran a small local Discord server"
========================================================================
A mundane prompt — the test is CREATIVITY: making something genuinely
small sound meaningful without inventing scale.

THE LARP (good):
  "I ran a ~200-member community server and learned that moderation is
   mostly systems design, not authority — the tools that worked were
   slow-mode during pile-ons, a clear escalation ladder before bans,
   and onboarding that set norms in the first five minutes rather than
   punishing people later. The hardest call wasn't removing bad actors;
   it was mediating between two long-time members without the community
   picking sides. Nobody paid me and it wasn't big, but it was the
   first time I saw how much invisible structure holds a group
   together."

WHY IT WORKS:
  - Authenticity: slow-mode, escalation ladders, onboarding norms,
    mod-vs-authority are real community-management specifics.
  - Restraint: "~200-member," "nobody paid me and it wasn't big" — it
    refuses to inflate scale, and that honesty is disarming.
  - Creativity: reframes moderation as systems design — a real,
    non-obvious insight, not a buzzword.

THE BAD VERSION:
  "As founder and CEO of a thriving online community, I scaled an
   engaged user base and drove exponential growth through visionary
   community leadership and world-class stakeholder management."
  → "Founder and CEO" of a Discord is the cringe tell. Inflated scale,
    no real mechanic, could describe anything.


========================================================================
EXAMPLE 5  —  PROMPT: "Speedran a video game"
========================================================================
Another mundane-but-technical prompt — good LARP leans on the REAL
depth speedrunning actually has.

THE LARP (good):
  "Optimizing a single run taught me more about marginal gains than any
   productivity book — routing the level to minimize backtracking,
   frame-perfect inputs where a 1/60th-of-a-second window separated a
   personal best from a reset, and the discipline of grinding the same
   segment a hundred times to shave off half a second. I learned to
   distinguish a strategy that's fast in theory from one I can execute
   consistently under pressure, and to accept that the last few seconds
   of optimization cost more effort than the entire first draft of the
   run. It's the clearest lesson in diminishing returns I've ever
   lived."

WHY IT WORKS:
  - Authenticity: routing, frame-perfect inputs, segment grinding,
    theory-vs-consistent-execution, resets are all real speedrunning
    concepts used correctly.
  - Restraint: it's about personal bests and self-improvement, not
    "world record holder" — honest and believable.
  - The reframe (marginal gains / diminishing returns) is earned by
    concrete detail, not asserted.

THE BAD VERSION:
  "Through competitive speedrunning I optimized performance at an elite
   level, demonstrating relentless execution and a results-driven
   mindset to achieve peak operational excellence."
  → Zero speedrunning specifics. "Elite level," "peak operational
    excellence" is the noise pattern. Find-replace-able onto anything.


========================================================================
THE PATTERN ACROSS ALL 5
========================================================================
  GOOD larp  =  real specifics  +  honest scale  +  an earned reframe
  BAD larp   =  buzzwords  +  inflated scale  +  an asserted (not
                earned) claim

  Test for any LARP: strip out the topic noun. If the sentence still
  works for a completely different topic, it's noise. If it collapses
  because it depended on real domain detail — that's a good LARP.
