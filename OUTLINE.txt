LARPedIn — SIMPLE BUILD OUTLINE (start to finish)
====================================================
Plain-language version of the plan. Tech details live in SPEC.md.
Clock: ~5 hours total. Steps in order, each one finishes before the next starts.

UPDATE (21:23): game is now SINGLE-PLAYER. No opponent, no "you win" screen.
You write your LARP, then the judging plays out like a COURTROOM SCENE —
the three judges argue about your LARP on screen, line by line — and at
the end the gavel drops: a rating out of 3000 (chess-style). Beat your
own best. Multiplayer battles = future roadmap, not tonight.

STEP 0 — SET UP THE PROJECT  (~30 min)
  Create the empty website project and define the basic building blocks:
  what a "prompt" is, what a "LARP" is, what a courtroom "verdict" looks
  like, what gets remembered about the player. Like drawing the game
  board before playing. Nothing works yet — it just exists and runs.

STEP 1 — MAKE THE COURTROOM FUNNY  (~1 hour)  ** MOST IMPORTANT STEP **
  Before building anything else, test the heart of the game: send a
  sample LARP to the AI and see if the 3 judges (Recruiter, VC, Intern)
  come back as an actual argument — distinct voices, disagreeing with
  each other, genuinely funny — plus fair scores. Test against the 5
  good/bad example pairs in LARP_EXAMPLES.txt: good ones must score
  high, bad ones low.
  GO/NO-GO GATE: if the argument is boring after an hour, simplify NOW —
  before any screens are built on top of it.

STEP 2 — SCORING MATH  (~20 min)
  Turn the judges' scores into the final rating out of 3000, work out
  which band you land in (from "Noise" up to "Grandmaster LARPer"), and
  save your personal best. Quick and boring on purpose.

STEP 3 — PUT IT ON THE INTERNET EARLY  (~30 min)
  Deploy a bare-bones version to a live URL now, with the AI call
  working server-side. Reason: fighting hosting problems at 11 PM is
  fine; fighting them at 1 AM kills the project.

STEP 4 — PROMPTS + EXAMPLES  (~40 min)
  Write ~10 game prompts across three difficulty tiers ("Clash Royale
  enthusiast" up to "You won ISEF") and a pre-written example LARP for
  each tier, so the text box is never blank. Save a couple of complete
  courtroom scenes so the game plays offline if the AI dies.
  (No fake opponents needed anymore — that whole chore died with
  multiplayer.)

STEP 5 — THE GAME SCREEN  (~1 hour)
  The playing flow: see a prompt, write your LARP in a pre-filled text
  box, hit submit, "court is convening…" — then into the courtroom.

STEP 6 — THE COURTROOM SCENE + THE LOOK  (~1 hour)  ** NEVER CUT **
  The showpiece. Judges' lines appear one by one like a courtroom drama —
  they argue, reference each other, roast the LARP. Each judge's score
  lands as they finish. Then the gavel: rating counts up to its final
  number out of 3000, your band is revealed, personal best updates.
  Dress it all in LinkedIn-parody style (corporate blue, fake
  "endorsements").

STEP 7 — FINAL 45 MINUTES  ** HARD STOP, NON-NEGOTIABLE **
  Record the demo video (best moment: a split courtroom — two judges
  impressed, one yelling it's fake — then the gavel), final pass on
  README + journal, push the code, submit on Devpost with screenshots.

ALREADY CUT (decided upfront, not if-we-run-out-of-time):

  - Fake opponents (obsolete — no battles)
  - Mobile polish
  - Per-round judge mood quirks

  2. Score history (keep personal best only)
  3. Third prompt tier (ship two)
  4. Type-on animation (lines appear whole; scene structure stays)

    scene still plays from cache.
  - Everything works solo — no second human needed to demo.
